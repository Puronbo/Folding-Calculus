## The Binding Energy Curve

Plot binding energy per nucleon against atomic mass number. The curve
rises steeply from hydrogen (∼1.1 MeV/nucleon for ²H) to a peak at
⁵⁶Fe (∼8.79 MeV/nucleon). Then it declines gradually through uranium
(∼7.57 MeV/nucleon).

This curve is the **topography of the nuclear crease**.

Iron sits at the bottom of the fold — the most tightly bound nucleus in
existence. Everything left of iron can release energy by fusing (moving
toward iron). Everything right of iron can release energy by fissioning
(also moving toward iron).

**The binding energy curve is a fold landscape, and all nuclear energy
comes from sliding down it.**

## Fusion: Climbing Over the Coulomb Crease

Two protons both have positive charge. They repel each other with a
Coulomb barrier of approximately 0.5–1 MeV. To fuse, they must approach
within about 1 femtometer — the range of the strong nuclear force.

The barrier is a **crease** in the potential energy landscape:

```
Energy
  ↑
  |    /\
  |   /  \     ← Coulomb barrier (the crease)
  |  /    \
  | /      \
  |/        \
  |          \_________  ← separated protons at infinite distance
  |           \
  |            \
  |             \______  ← bound state (strong force well)
  |
  +------------------------→ Separation distance
```

The two protons must cross this crease to reach the bound state.
Classically, they cannot — the barrier is ∼1000 times higher than the
thermal energy in the Sun's core (∼1.3 keV). But quantum tunneling
allows them to pass *through* the crease, emerging on the other side
where the strong force binds them.

**The crease is the Coulomb barrier. Tunneling is crossing the crease.
Binding is engaging the fold.**

Each step in the proton-proton chain is a crease crossing:

| Reaction | Barrier | Energy released |
|----------|---------|-----------------|
| p + p → ²H + e⁺ + νₑ | 0.5 MeV | 1.44 MeV |
| ²H + p → ³He + γ | 0.5 MeV | 5.49 MeV |
| ³He + ³He → ⁴He + 2p | ∼1 MeV | 12.86 MeV |

Total: 26.73 MeV per ⁴He nucleus produced. The energy comes from the
mass defect — the folded binding energy released when the fold tightens.

## Fission: Tearing the Crease

A heavy nucleus like ²³⁵U is like a stretched fold. The short-range
strong force binds it, but the long-range Coulomb repulsion between
protons strains it. When a neutron is absorbed, the nucleus deforms
like a liquid drop. The neck narrows. The two lobes separate.

This is **tearing the crease**.

The scission point is where the strong force can no longer hold the neck.
Coulomb repulsion between the two fragments accelerates them apart,
converting binding energy into kinetic energy. About 200 MeV per
fission — the fold releasing its stored energy.

**If fusion is engaging the fold, fission is it tearing open.**

## E = mc² as the Fold Conversion Ratio

The energy released in both fusion and fission comes from the **mass
defect** — the mass of the bound nucleus is less than the sum of its
constituent nucleons. The missing mass (multiplied by c²) is the
binding energy.

The factor c² appears twice in physics:
1. In E = mc² — the mass-energy equivalence
2. In the Minkowski metric — converting time to a spatial length

This is not a coincidence. The same constant sets the scale for both:

$$ds² = -c² dt² + dx² + dy² + dz²$$

$$E = mc²$$

Mass is **folded spacetime**. When a nucleus binds, some of the mass
disappears — the spacetime in that region has been folded more tightly.
When the nucleus splits or fuses, the fold relaxes toward the iron
minimum, and the excess energy appears as kinetic energy of the
fragments — **unfolded spacetime**.

The binding energy curve **is** the curve of the spacetime fold at the
nuclear scale.

## The Strong Force as the Fold Agent

What creates the fold? The strong nuclear force.

The nucleon-nucleon potential has a characteristic shape:
- A repulsive core at r < 0.5 fm (you cannot fold too tightly)
- An attractive well from 0.5 to 2.5 fm (the fold binds)
- An exponential tail beyond 2.5 fm (the fold relaxes)

This potential well is the **fold** in the nuclear medium. The strong
force is what bends the energy landscape into a well. The depth of the
well (∼50 MeV per nucleon) sets the scale of the fold.

The binding energy curve maps how this fold changes with nucleus size.
For small nuclei (left of iron), the fold is shallow because each
nucleon has few neighbors. As the nucleus grows, the fold deepens —
more neighbors, more binding. But at iron, the Coulomb repulsion begins
to dominate. For large nuclei, the fold is stretched and strained until
it tears.

## Summary: The Nuclear Fold Landscape

| Region | Behavior | Fold analogy |
|--------|----------|--------------|
| Left of iron (H → Fe) | Fusion releases energy | Sliding downhill into deeper fold |
| Iron (⁵⁶Fe) | Maximum binding | Bottom of the fold — most stable |
| Right of iron (Fe → U) | Fission releases energy | Sliding downhill from stretched fold |
| Neutron capture | Adding to the fold | Folding tighter until it tears |
| Quantum tunneling | Crossing the barrier | Passing through the crease |

The binding energy curve is not just a plot of nuclear data. It is the
topography of the crease at the nuclear scale — the shape of the fold
that the strong force carves into the sheet of spacetime.
