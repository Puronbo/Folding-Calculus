# Language and Logic: Creases in Symbolic Space

## 1. The Saussurean Sign as a Crease

Ferdinand de Saussure's signifier/signified distinction exhibits the
90° crease structure:

- **Fold (J):** The signifier — the sound or written form (the fold
  from concept to expression).
- **Unfold (∫):** The signified — the concept itself (the integral
  that reconstructs meaning from form).
- **Crease (Nⱼ ≠ 0):** The arbitrary nature of the sign — the gap
  between signifier and signified is a non-integrable crease.
  There is no necessary connection between "dog" and the concept
  of a dog. This non-integrability is what makes language
  symbolic rather than iconic.

**Evidence:** Saussure's principle of the arbitrariness of the sign
is the founding observation of structural linguistics. Onomatopoeia
(iconic signs) are the rare cases where integrability nearly holds
(Nⱼ ≈ 0).

## 2. Metaphor as a Crease Crossing

Lakoff and Johnson (1980) show that metaphor is the crease between
conceptual domains:

- **Fold:** Source domain — the concrete, physical domain that
  provides structure.
- **Unfold:** Target domain — the abstract domain being understood.
- **Crease:** The metaphor itself — the mapping that projects
  source-domain structure onto the target. The cross-domain
  mapping is non-integrable: not every feature of the source
  transfers. The Nijenhuis tensor of the metaphorical mapping
  is non-zero at the crease.

**Example:** "Argument is war." We win and lose arguments, we
attack positions, we defend claims. But arguments are not literally
war — the mapping is partial. The non-integrability is what makes
metaphor creative rather than literal.

## 3. Logical Paradoxes

The liar paradox ("This statement is false") is a crease in logic:

- **Fold:** Assertion — the act of making a truth-claim (the fold
  from thought to statement).
- **Unfold:** Truth evaluation — the process of verifying the claim.
- **Crease (Nⱼ ≠ 0):** Self-reference — where the statement refers
  to itself. The truth predicate cannot be consistently integrated
  (Tarski's undefinability theorem). The crease is the fixed point
  of the diagonal lemma.

**Parallel:** Gödel's incompleteness theorems: any sufficiently
powerful formal system contains a statement that is true but
unprovable. This statement is the crease — where the system's
consistency cannot be integrated into the system itself.

## 4. Language Change (Sound Laws)

- **Fold:** A sound shift — a discrete, exceptionless change in
  pronunciation (e.g., Grimm's Law: p → f, t → θ, k → h).
- **Unfold:** Analogical leveling — the gradual regularization
  of paradigms.
- **Crease:** The individual lexical item where the sound change
  fails to apply — the "exception that proves the rule."

**The Neogrammarian controversy:** Do sound changes operate without
exception (Neogrammarian position) or with lexical diffusion
(Wang 1969)? The answer: the crease. Sound laws hold exactly where
Nⱼ = 0 (no morphological conditioning). Exceptions occur at the
crease (Nⱼ ≠ 0) where morphological and phonological systems
interact.
