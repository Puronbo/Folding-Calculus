# Economics: The Crease in Markets and Money

## 1. Market Crashes as Cusp Catastrophes

Financial markets exhibit the telltale signature of the cusp catastrophe:

- **Fold (J):** The crash — a 90° discontinuity in price. The market's
  fundamental value (the stable branch) suddenly folds to a new level.
- **Unfold (∫):** Recovery — the long, slow process of price discovery and
  mean reversion. The integral of investor confidence over time.
- **Crease (Nⱼ ≠ 0):** The circuit breaker threshold or margin call level —
  the point where the market's Nijenhuis tensor (the failure of arbitrage
  to enforce consistency) becomes non-zero.

**Evidence:** Zeeman (1974) proposed the cusp catastrophe as a model of
stock market crashes. Empirical support: trading halts occur at pre-defined
percentage drops (the bifurcation set). Price distributions show bimodality
(before/after crash), characteristic of cusp behavior.

## 2. Business Cycles

- **Fold:** Recession — rapid contraction (the fold from expansion to
  contraction).
- **Unfold:** Recovery — slow expansionary unfolding.
- **Crease:** The "turning point" — the NBER-defined peak or trough where
  growth changes sign. This is the crease in the business cycle.

## 3. Innovation Adoption

The Bass diffusion model is a cusp catastrophe in adoption space:

- **Fold:** Early adopters trigger the rapid growth phase (the fold "over
  the chasm").
- **Unfold:** Market saturation — the integral of adoption over time approaches
  the carrying capacity.
- **Crease:** The "chasm" (Moore 1991) — the threshold between early adopters
  and early majority. Innovations that fail to cross this crease die.

**Data:** S-curves of technology adoption consistently show a critical
threshold (10–15% market share) beyond which adoption becomes self-sustaining
(Rogers 1962, Moore 1991). This is the crease in diffusion space.

## 4. Money as an Integral

- **Fold:** Currency creation — the act of minting or issuing money (the fold
  that brings money into existence).
- **Unfold:** Currency destruction — taxation, inflation, or demonetization.
- **Crease:** The point of monetary regime change — e.g., the gold standard
  suspension, the transition to fiat money, or the introduction of
  cryptocurrency.

## 5. Behavioral Economics: The Endowment Effect

The endowment effect (Kahneman, Thaler) shows that losses are felt ~2× more
than equivalent gains. This is a crease at the reference point:

- The value function V(x) has a kink at x = 0 (the reference point).
- The derivative switches from ~2 (loss domain) to ~1 (gain domain).
- This is exactly a ReLU-like crease in prospect theory.
