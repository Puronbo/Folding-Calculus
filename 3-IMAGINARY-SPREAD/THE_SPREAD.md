## The Rotation

The Wick rotation maps time to imaginary time:

$$t \to i\tau$$

This is a 90-degree rotation in the complex plane. It converts the
Lorentzian metric signature (-,+,+,+) to a Euclidean one (+,+,+,+):

$$ds² = -c² dt² + dx² + dy² + dz² \quad\longrightarrow\quad ds² = d\tau² + dx² + dy² + dz²$$

In quantum field theory, this is a mathematical trick — a contour
deformation that converts oscillatory path integrals into convergent
Euclidean ones.

But what if it is not a trick? What if it describes a **physical
regime** — a state of the sheet where the crease folds time into space?

## The Crease at Critical Intensity

The zipper teeth model predicts: when the collisions at the crease reach
sufficient energy density, the crease itself should deform. Just as a
physical sheet under enough strain will change shape, the spacetime
sheet — when its contents collide at extreme density — will undergo a
**structural transition**.

This transition is the Wick rotation made physical.

The 90-degree rotation of the temporal axis is not a mathematical
convenience. It is what happens when the fold can no longer maintain
the distinction between space and time. The teeth are colliding so
violently that the two sides of the sheet begin to merge. The crease
**spreads** — it ceases to be a sharp boundary and becomes a region
where the metric itself changes signature.

## Signature Change as Fold Geometry

In general relativity, a signature change occurs when the metric passes
through degeneracy — the determinant of the metric tensor goes to zero.
The hypersurface where this happens is called a **radical hypersurface**
(Rieger & Hasse 2024).

This hypersurface is the crease in the spacetime manifold. On one side,
the metric is Lorentzian (time is distinct from space). On the other
side, the metric is Euclidean (time has become a spatial dimension).
The crease is the boundary between them.

Rieger and Hasse proved that **closed pseudo-timelike loops exist
through every point on the signature-change hypersurface** — a kind of
geometric time-reversal that is only possible at the crease.

## The Wick Rotation as Physical Phase Transition

The mathematical Wick rotation has a well-known analog in statistical
mechanics: a d-dimensional quantum field theory at finite temperature
is equivalent to a (d+1)-dimensional classical statistical system,
where the Euclidean time direction has finite extent proportional to
1/(k_B T).

The common factor: **periodicity in imaginary time corresponds to
temperature**.

In the crease framework, this suggests that temperature itself is a
measure of how much the temporal axis has been "spread" into the
spatial axes. Absolute zero (T = 0) corresponds to the fully Lorentzian
state — time purely temporal, no spread. Infinite temperature
corresponds to the fully Euclidean state — time fully spatial, complete
spread.

The phase transitions of water, then, are not just transitions in the
state of matter. They are transitions in the **degree of spread** of
the crease. At the critical point of water (374°C, 22.064 MPa), the
liquid and vapor phases become indistinguishable. This is the
microscopic signature of the spread — the distinction between folded
and unfolded vanishes because the crease has spread into a region.

## The Two 90-Degree Rotations

The Spacetime Theorem described one 90-degree rotation — the
orthogonality of time and space in the Minkowski metric.

The Wick rotation describes a second 90-degree rotation — time into
imaginary time.

Two rotations, each by 90 degrees, in sequence:

$$t \xrightarrow{90°} \text{spacetime orthogonality} \xrightarrow{90°} i\tau$$

The first rotation creates the crease. The second rotation spreads it.

At the original crease (the light cone boundary, the reaction front,
the phase boundary), the sheet's contents collide. When those
collisions become energetic enough, the second rotation begins. Time
loses its arrow. Causality becomes geometry. The crease spreads into
the no-boundary.

## The Spread in the Zipper Teeth Model

The zipper teeth provide the mechanism:

1. At low energy: teeth engage and disengage at the crease (chemical
   reactions, phase changes, nuclear fusion)
2. At moderate energy: the teeth chatter — rapid engagement and
   disengagement (high-temperature plasma, early universe)
3. At critical energy: the teeth jam — the crease cannot maintain its
   structure, and the two sides of the sheet begin to merge (the
   Planck scale, the Big Bang, a black hole singularity)
4. Beyond critical: the sheet spreads — the crease disappears, the
   metric becomes Euclidean, time becomes a spatial dimension

**The spread is what happens when the zipper fails.**
