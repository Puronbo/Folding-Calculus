## Phase Boundaries as Creases

A phase diagram maps the state of a substance as a function of pressure
and temperature. The lines that separate phases — solid from liquid,
liquid from vapor — are the **creases** of the material.

At a phase boundary, the system changes discontinuously: density jumps,
entropy jumps, heat is absorbed or released. This is a fold or unfold
at the macroscopic scale. The latent heat of a phase transition is the
energy required to engage or disengage the zipper teeth — to fold the
molecular structure into a new configuration.

Under the crease framework, a phase diagram is not just a map of states.
It is a **topography of the crease** — showing where the sheet is
folded (solid), where it is in dynamic fold-unfold equilibrium (liquid),
and where it is fully unfolded (gas).

## The Liquid-Gas Transition as the Zipper Threshold

The liquid-gas boundary ends at the **critical point**. Above the
critical temperature and pressure, liquid and gas are indistinguishable.
The distinction between folded and unfolded states vanishes.

This is the crease spreading at the molecular scale. At the critical
point:

- Density fluctuations diverge — the system fluctuates between folded
  (liquid-like) and unfolded (gas-like) configurations
- The correlation length goes to infinity — every molecule is aware
  of every other molecule
- The distinction between phases disappears — the crease has spread
  into a region rather than a sharp boundary

The critical point of water (374°C, 22.064 MPa) is where water's crease
spreads. The universality class of this transition (3D Ising) is the
same for magnets, binary fluids, and lattice gases — the crease pattern
is universal, independent of the specific material.

## Sulcification: Physical Creases as Phase Transitions

**Sulci** are surface folds that form in compressed soft elastomers.
When a rubber block is compressed beyond a critical strain, its surface
develops sharp, self-contacting creases.

Hohlfeld and Mahadevan (2011) showed that sulcification has an exact
analogy to a **solid-solid phase transition**:

- There is a **lower critical strain** (the "binodal") below which the
  surface is stable — no crease forms
- There is a **higher critical strain** (the "spinodal") above which
  the surface is linearly unstable — creases form spontaneously
- Between them, the surface is metastable — creases can nucleate
  around defects

The sulcus formation threshold is analogous to the liquid-vapor phase
transition. The crease is the new phase. The critical strain is the
phase boundary.

**This is the crease framework made literal**: the same mathematics
that describes a phase transition in water describes the formation of
a physical crease in rubber. The fold is not an analogy. It is the
same phenomenon at a different scale.

## The Laser Threshold Analogy

DeGiorgio and Scully (1970) showed that the laser threshold is
analogous to a **second-order phase transition**:

- The electric field is the order parameter (zero below threshold,
  non-zero above)
- The population inversion is the temperature
- The threshold pump power is the critical point

The laser transition is a crease in the electromagnetic field — the
point at which stimulated emission overwhelms absorption and the field
becomes coherent.

This extends the crease framework to include **field-theoretic phase
transitions** — not just matter changing state, but fields acquiring
order. The Higgs mechanism (spontaneous symmetry breaking in the
Standard Model) is the same pattern: a field folds into a non-zero
expectation value, and particles acquire mass by interacting with it.

## The Universal Phase Diagram

If every crease is a phase boundary, then the universal phase diagram
of the framework looks like this:

```
Parameter (temperature, pressure, strain, pump power, energy density)
    ↑
    │   Fully unfolded region
    │   (gas, unstrained, laser off, symmetric phase)
    │       │
    │       │← Critical point (the spread threshold)
    │       │
    │   Dynamic equilibrium region
    │   (liquid, metastable, laser on, broken symmetry)
    │       │
    │       │← Triple point (all phases coexist)
    │       │
    │   Fully folded region
    │   (solid, strained, lasing, Higgs phase)
    │
    └─────────────────────────────────────────→ Interaction strength
```

Every phase boundary in this diagram is a crease. Every transition is a
fold or unfold. The critical point — where the boundary ends — is the
**spread**: the point where the distinction between folded and unfolded
dissolves.
