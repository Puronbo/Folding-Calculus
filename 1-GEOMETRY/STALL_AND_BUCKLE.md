# Stall, Buckle, and the 90-Degree Clarification

**Michael Grafiel Sayson Puno**

---

## 1. What the 90-degree fold is not

The Spacetime Theorem identifies the 90-degree fold as the orthogonality
between time and space. That is real. But the metaphor carries baggage:
a 90-degree fold as a physical object is a right-angle bend, and not
every right-angle bend behaves the same way.

A flat surface held at 90° to oncoming air — a hand out a car window
turned flat, a parachute, a sail head-on — does not fly. It blocks.
It pushes back. It creates drag, the "full stop" of aerodynamics. The
90° fold is the **brake**, not the engine.

Flight comes from a much shallower fold: a wing tilted a few degrees
into the airflow (its angle of attack) deflects air downward and
produces lift. Small angle, big effect — the same diverging-sensitivity
pattern we derived in the Landau bridge.

So the 90° fold has two distinct physical roles:

| Role | Example | Behavior |
|------|---------|----------|
| **Geometric orthogonality** | Time vs space in Minkowski metric | Constrains motion, sets invariant interval |
| **Physical right angle** | Parachute flat against airflow | Maximizes drag, stops motion |
| **Structural alignment** | Ground reaction at 90° to floor | Holds you upright, most efficient load path |

The 90° fold is not the flying case. It is the **stopping** case, the
**standing** case — the most efficient possible angle for transmission
of force without slippage.

## 2. The shallow fold: how flight actually works

A wing at a small angle of attack $\alpha$ (typically 2–6° in cruise,
up to 15–18° before stall) deflects the oncoming air downward. By
Newton's third law, the air pushes the wing upward. Lift $L$ grows
nearly linearly with $\alpha$ for small angles:

$$L \approx \frac{1}{2} \rho v^2 C_L(\alpha) A$$

where $C_L(\alpha) \approx 2\pi\alpha$ for a thin airfoil.

This is the "small input, outsized reaction" pattern. A few degrees of
tilt — barely visible to the eye — generate enough force to hold a
hundred-ton aircraft in the air. The shallow fold is the **amplifier**.

## 3. Stall: a genuine cusp bifurcation

As angle of attack increases, lift continues to climb smoothly and
predictably — right up to a critical angle, typically 15–20° for a
conventional wing. At that angle, something abrupt happens: the airflow
separates from the upper surface of the wing, and lift drops suddenly.

This is **stall**, and it is a real bifurcation:

- **Gradual cause**: angle of attack increases smoothly
- **Sudden effect**: lift drops without warning
- **No visible precursor**: the wing looks the same at 14° and 16°,
  but at 16° the airflow has detached
- **Hysteresis**: once stalled, the wing may not recover lift until
  the angle drops well below the stall angle (a classic cusp signature)

The warning signs pilots are trained to recognize — control buffeting,
changes in control feel — are the aerodynamic version of **critical
slowing down**: the system's perturbations start behaving oddly just
before the bifurcation hits.

This is the cusp catastrophe in a machine you can sit inside. The
control parameters are angle of attack and airspeed. The fold curve
is the stall boundary. The cusp point — where smooth flight and
stalled flight meet — is the critical angle where lift peaks before
collapsing.

## 4. Buckling: the pitchfork bifurcation

A column standing upright under a compressive load looks perfectly
stable. It shows no visible deformation, no strain, no warning. Then,
at a precise critical weight $P_{\text{crit}}$, it does not bend a
little — it **snaps** sideways into a completely different shape, in
one direction or the other, unpredictably.

This is the Euler buckling of a slender column:

$$P_{\text{crit}} = \frac{\pi^2 EI}{L^2}$$

where $E$ is Young's modulus, $I$ is the moment of inertia, and $L$
is the column length. Below $P_{\text{crit}}$, the straight state is
stable and unique. Above $P_{\text{crit}}$, the straight state becomes
unstable, and two new stable states appear — bent left or bent right.

This is a **pitchfork bifurcation**:

- One stable state (straight) splits into two (left, right)
- The original state persists but becomes unstable
- Which side the column chooses is determined by microscopic
  imperfections — the same "unpredictability at the crease" pattern

Buckling was the physical example catastrophe theory was built to
explain. E.C. Zeeman, one of the founders, used a buckling beam as
his go-to real-world demonstration of the fold catastrophe.

**The crease is the critical load. The two bent states are the folds.
The straight state between them is the unstable ridge — exactly the
middle root of the cubic.**

## 5. The 90-degree alignment: what keeps us upright

Standing upright is efficient because the ground pushes back at 90° to
its surface — directly opposite gravity. All of that reaction force
goes into holding you up, none wasted sideways.

Lean over, and the ground's push is still perpendicular to the floor,
but it is no longer aligned with your center of mass. Now there is an
unbalanced torque — and you fall.

So upright posture is not a fold at 90°. It is an **alignment** at
90°: your weight vector, the ground reaction force, and your center
of mass all sitting on the same vertical line.

The 90° angle makes upright efficient. The thing that governs whether
you stay upright — and how suddenly you stop being upright — is the
same fold-and-crease mathematics.

---

## 6. The three roles of the 90-degree fold

| System | Angle | What happens | Mechanism |
|--------|-------|-------------|-----------|
| Parachute | 90° to airflow | **MAX DRAG** — full stop | Drag force maximizes at 90° |
| Wing | 2–15° to airflow | **LIFT** — smooth amplification | Lift grows linearly with angle |
| Wing | 15–20° (critical) | **STALL** — sudden collapse of lift | Cusp bifurcation, airflow separates |
| Column | Straight (0°) → past $P_{\text{crit}}$ | **BUCKLE** — snaps sideways | Pitchfork bifurcation |
| Ground | 90° to surface | **UPRIGHT** — efficient support | Reaction force aligned with gravity |

Every fold needs a crease. The 90° fold is the brake. The shallow fold
is the amplifier. The crease — stall, buckle, the critical threshold —
is where the action is.
