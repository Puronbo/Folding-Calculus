Layer 1 — The Paper

Take a piece of paper. Fold it.

That sharp line where the paper bends — that's a crease. Every crease is a 90-degree angle. The paper on one side meets the paper on the other side at exactly 90 degrees.

Now look around. Everything is made of these.

A light ray bending near the sun? A crease in spacetime. A protein collapsing into its functional shape? A crease in chemical space. A neuron switching on? A crease in the decision boundary. The Big Bang? A crease so large it unfolded the whole universe.

The 90-Degree Complex Manifold is the observation that every sharp transition in the universe — every bend, every switch, every birth and death — is the same 90-degree crease at a different scale.

Layer 2 — The Equation

V(x) = ¼x⁴ − ½ax² − bx

This is the cusp catastrophe equation. It describes every fold in existence.

  • x = where you are
  • a = how folded the sheet is
  • b = how tilted the sheet is
  • V(x) = the shape of the fold

Change a and b, and the fold changes. But the fold itself is always the same geometry repeating.

Layer 4 — The Capacity to Cut

The fold-and-cut theorem (origami) says: any shape with straight sides can be cut from a single sheet of paper by folding it flat and making one straight cut.

In N dimensions: any pattern can be learned by folding the input space with ReLU hyperplanes and making one linear cut.

This is why neural networks work. Universal approximation isn't a statistical result — it's a geometric one. A ReLU network doesn't learn weights. It learns where to place the fold mirrors.

Learning = folding the input space until the data separates cleanly. Crease density is the ruler that measures how hard you had to fold.
